I very recently learned about the term "stream of consciousness writing," and I thought it was a pretty neat premise. Instead of planning a piece from the beginning, outlining and slowly fleshing out an idea, stream of consciousness writing attempts to emulate the way our brains think. Similarly linked is something like discovery writing where you write without an outline. You plant a seed and see what grows from it.

Both of these are narrative techniques that are used for creating written pieces. There's some more nuances, like stream of consciousness writing sometimes shirking proper grammar or discovery writing sometimes lacking a solid middle, but for the sake of this piece, I think of them as all one thing where one writes whatever comes to mind.

After I learned about this, it was a bit of an "aha" moment for me. I realized that when I write my blog posts, this is how I write. When I write, I typically just start with the idea in the form of the title. I have no idea where I'm going next. For example, the title of this article is "Stream of Consciousness Writing." Obviously I am trying to fit what I write around that theme, but I don't really have an idea for where I am going to go. I am just exploring the idea, and in turn, I end up thinking more deeply about it.

Because that is how I think. To be able to think about something, I need to be able to sort through my ideas. Writing about something, and putting that stream of thought onto a page allows my brain to organize the ideas and really internalize what I think of them. It's almost the same as how teaching a topic to someone allows you to learn more yourself. Teaching forces you to explain every individual step, and writing is the same way. If my stream of consciousness was merely left inside my head, I may skip over portions that I do not think are important to explore. By writing, however, I have to methodically mention every step, and oftentimes it turns out that a step I didn't think was important before turns out to have much more depth than I initially realized.

This is somewhat of an interruption to the natural stream of consciousness I have, but I still put this style of writing into that category. Despite my line of thinking changing depending on if it is internal or if it is being relayed to another, the core principle is the same: I am writing my thought process.

I do think it is important though to be able to understand our own writing styles, and what voice we use with our words. I have a separate blog post specifically about writing that I will be releasing in the future. If we don't understand our style of writing, then we'll be at risk of trying to fit into a system that doesn't fit with us. The words we use won't give the message that we mean to send.

So this post was mostly a short little thought exercise in exploring my own writing style. I write with a more thoughtful narrative pace, and as I explore writing more (for more technical writing, fiction novels, short stories), I have been thinking a lot about how I can apply this style of writing into different avenues. I don't quite have the answer yet, but I do hope that these recent developments I've had will get me halfway there.

And even if it doesn't help me get over the writer's block struggles I sometimes have, then at the very least I have learned a little bit more about myself, and that's what it's all about in the end.
