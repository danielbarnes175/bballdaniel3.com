---
title: "Privacy is a right"
date: "2026-04-22"
tags: ["general", "privacy"]
---
When I was a freshman in college, I took an English course. As part of this, we had to do an analysis of Daniel Solove's "Nothing to Hide", argument. Eessentially we had to determine if it was a valid argument or not. What are the pros and cons? Where is he right, and where do people commonly push back? At the time, I didn't really have any skin in the game. I didn't really understand why people cared so much. And it was for a school assignment, so I just wanted to get through it with as little effort as possible.

For those of you that don't know, the nothing to hide argument is essentially saying "why should you care about privacy if you have nothing to hide," and Solove provided numerous arguments against that. At its core, it's a discussion about privacy. And years after I first read his writings, it seems that the rise of surveillance is increasing day by day. More than ever, it seems pertinent to say: privacy is a right.

In the old day, before the digital landscape we live in now, privacy was something that came naturally. There may have been attempts to remove privacy in various portions of people's lives, but ultimately, it was easy to keep yourself hidden. Even for things where they kept physical records, for that to get out, it either had to have been spread via word of mouth, or by someone having physical access to the records, both vectors that had lower chances of spread and lower risk of actually happening.

Nowadays, we live in this digital landscape where everything is kept online. An old adage people stopped saying at some point, despite it still remaining true, is "once something is on the internet, it is there forever," and now these attack vectors for breaking our privacy are much more prevelant. No longer does one have to be physically next to your medical records to read them. No longer does someone need to be told by you to find out where you live. Now, everyone collects your data. Everyone has access to it, whether by purchasing it through somehow legal means, or by more nefarious means.

The entire point of why privacy needs to remain a right is because we cannot trust the entities that are supposed to keep it safe. Oftentimes we think that our data is only in the hands of the people we give it to. We may trust them to hold our data, but even with the highest level of trust, it does not make them impervious. When the companies aren't willfully selling your data, data breaches have become increasingly more common. And suddenly, the whole world might have access to knowing about that weird rash you have, or your stalker might find you even after you move.

It's a bit of a slippery slope argument, but one cannot deny that there is a slope in the first place.

And yet, despite this, most people don't really care. We'll happily trade our privacy when it comes with a fancy service attached. We'll gladly let someone learn more about our habits than what we know ourselves when it's packaged in a nice box. We'll agree to your terms and conditions without even for a second thought thinking about what they even say.

And maybe that's ok. Maybe it's fine if a large company knows about what websites I like to visit in my free time, or what games I like to play. Maybe it's fine for a big store to know exactly how much time I'm spending in an aisle looking at specific products, and be able to later send me a coupon for said product. Maybe it's fine to start receiving ads for baby clothes when you haven't yet announced your pregnancy. None of this really hurts us, and hey, maybe I'll even get some value out of it. And who cares if that data is leaked?

Who cares if these companies need to know about us to make a better product. I mean, they are making a better product, right?

I think that's a decision everybody has to make for themselves. But that is the main crux of the argument; it should be a decision. More often nowadays we are opted-in by default into these vast data collection workflows and policies. We don't get a choice.

Everyone would agree that they don't want people peering through their back window into their home, so to some extent we all have some measure of privacy we want in our lives. That fundamental reason is why privacy is a right. People will have different levels of privacy needs and wants, and it should be up to the individual to decide for themself what that level should be for them.

Instead, that right to decide the level of privacy someone wants is being taken away. We are getting less and less of a choice in the matter as the days pass. We aren't able to decide our own risks and keep ourselves safe from them. We have lost that autonomy in our own lives. Couldn't anyone agree that taking away someone's autonomy is taking away their rights? And at what point does taking away someone's rights go too far?

These are the questions that I don't have an answer to. They're the questions that are constantly up for debate. For me though, I just know that privacy is not one of those questions that should be up for debate.
